Sir Alan Frisbee - Bio

Sir Alan Frisbee is an enigma of the analog age. He was born in a one-room shack in Raccoon, Kentucky, as his grandmother, Bertha May, led a Sacred Harp chorus around the birthing bed. Alan crowned just as the fuguing tunes reached their crescendo. Music has been a part of his daily life ever since. At the age of five, Alan won the Cream of Kentucky Holler Yodeling Competition, defeating 40-year-old ten-time champion Rondey "The Throat" McClucky. He remains the youngest winner on record. In 1975, Alan met Dan Child, whose family was traveling with a circus that happened to pass through town. Alan saw Dan playing a steel-string guitar and immediately asked him to start a band. Alan recruited his brother Rick on drums, and The Pelts were born.

Alan and Dan spent a year shaping their sound—an unlikely fusion of bluegrass, folk, and a raw, new kind of rock and roll. After assembling about thirty minutes of material, they recorded their debut album Pelted in a single take at a local radio station’s bluegrass studio. Eager to be heard, they mailed the tape to anyone who might listen. Rick, whose sideline in low-level drug dealing took him to nearby cities, used his connections in the music scene to quietly pass along copies. One night in Richmond, Virginia, he slipped a tape to the manager of Flo & Eddie. A week later, Alan got a call: if they could pay their way, The Pelts were invited to open for Flo & Eddie in South Bend, Indiana.



In the mid-'90s, Alan was knighted by the King of the tiny, obscure island nation of *Marzipan* (located somewhere in the South Pacific, but no one can quite agree on where). The King, a fan of Alan's music, declared him "Sir Alan Frisbee" in a lavish, yet private ceremony — reportedly, it involved a lot of fireworks and one large goat. Alan has worn the title ever since, though no official records exist, and most historians agree it was an unorthodox honor.

Alan claims to be the first man to sing falsetto in a rock context — a claim not widely verified, but fiercely defended. He also insists he invented the "dudun" guitar line in *Jack & Diane* while sitting in on a session with John Mellencamp, though no tapes confirm this.

Alan does not use computers. This site was originally built by second cousin Tom Diskiss, a fan and reluctant webmaster, who only agreed to hand over the domain after Alan showed up to a family reunion in 2004. Tom still updates the site today, under Alan's supervision — which is mostly done by fax.
